# MANDATE
### A Civic Roguelike of Listening, Trust, and the Long Disaster

---

## Premise

You govern New York City. You are told a disaster will arrive in 100 years. You will not be in office when it comes — but the city you shape will face it. *Mandate* is not about preventing the disaster. It is about deciding **what kind of city survives it**, and whether the bonds between people are strong enough to hold when it does.

The core question the game asks: **Do you build a city that is rich, or a city that takes care of itself?** And what does each of those cities become when the storm finally hits?

---

## Design DNA

*Mandate* braids three games together, taking a different layer from each:

| Source | What we take | How it shows up in Mandate |
|--------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Frostpunk** | Moral weight and hidden consequence | Visible meters (Trust, Money) sit on top of hidden meters (Gentrification, Surveillance, Community Power) that only surface mid-term through the world |
| **Disco Elysium** | Insight through dialogue; departments as *lenses* | You unlock policies only by gaining insights through conversation. Funding a department changes what you *see* and what NPCs will talk to you about |
| **Balatro** | Run-based discovery of synergies | Each term is a run. Policies combine into emergent effects. Rare unlocks carry forward and reshape future runs |

The thesis tying them together: **you discover department and policy synergies (Balatro), those synergies carry hidden moral consequences (Frostpunk), and those consequences reshape how the city speaks to you in future terms (Disco Elysium).**

---

## The Two Resources

Everything in *Mandate* flows from a single tension:

- **Time** is what you *spend*. It is the scarce resource. Every day you have a limited number of meeting slots, and choosing one constituency means ignoring another.
- **Trust** and **Money** are what you *accumulate* — and they pull against each other.

Helping people costs money but builds trust. A high-trust city is one where **people take care of each other** — community aid flows, neighbors look out for neighbors, the social fabric holds. A high-money, low-trust city is wealthy but **atomized** — everyone looks out for themselves, and the social fabric quietly erodes, which corrupts decision-making down the line.

Neither path is "correct." They produce **different cities that survive the disaster in different ways** — or fail to.

---

## The Hidden Meters

Beneath Trust and Money sit three meters the player **cannot see directly**:

1. **Gentrification** — rises when housing and finance policies stack without counterbalance
2. **Surveillance** — rises from policing and monitoring policies
3. **Community Power** — rises from welfare, education, and participatory policies; the prerequisite for many disaster-survival options

You don't read these on a number. You learn about them **through the world** — an NPC who was warm starts mentioning rising rents; a community organizer begins talking about displacement; long-time residents describe being pushed out. This is the Frostpunk lesson: *you realize what you've been building only when it's already built.*

---

## The Core Loop: The Three-Phase Week

A term lasts **12 weeks**. Each week moves through three phases.

### Phase 1 — Meetings (Listening)
You have **six meeting slots per day**, but you cannot take every meeting. Which meetings *surface* is **semi-random but rule-governed**: financiers appear more in certain weeks, community organizers cluster after unrest spikes, environmental groups surface when pollution climbs. You choose who to sit with — Harlem, Central Manhattan, Wall Street — and every choice locks out the others.

Conversations are where **insights** are mined. You don't know what insight an NPC holds until you talk to them, and even then, surfacing a **rare insight** is *non-deterministic* — there's a chance per conversation, improved by digging deeper, asking the right questions, or having the right department lens active.

### Phase 2 — Reflection (Seeing)
You step back and look **holistically**. The **city map** has updated to reflect what you learned: neighborhoods shift color as tension rises, community centers appear where you invested, neglected boroughs show strain. This is where you notice you spent all week in Manhattan and never once went to the Bronx — and what that cost you.

### Phase 3 — Policy Building (Deciding)
You **spend insights** to unlock and enact policies. This is the deck-builder layer. You can only build policies you have the insights for — no housing conversations means no housing policy, no matter how badly you need it. Policies combine: **Education + Community Networks + high Trust** might unlock a latent capability you won't appreciate until the disaster arrives.

---

## Insights → Policies → City

The progression spine:

**Insights** are gained through conversation. They are the *currency of unlocking*.
**Policies** are bought with insights. They are the *actions you take on the city*.
**The Map** is reshaped by policies. It is the *feedback that tells you what you've neglected*.

This closes a clean loop: *meetings shape insights → insights unlock policies → policies reshape the map → the map reveals what to address in next week's meetings.*

Because you can't choose every meeting and can't guarantee every insight, **no two terms give you the same policy palette.** A term dominated by finance meetings builds a structurally different city than one spent listening to organizers.

---

## Departments as Lenses

Departments are not just resource sinks — they are **ways of seeing**, borrowed from Disco Elysium's Thought Cabinet. Funding the Environment Department doesn't only unlock environmental policies; it makes you *notice* environmental problems in conversations and opens new dialogue with NPCs who care about them.

Departments can also **argue with each other**. When a decision comes up, your Housing department and your Finance department may pull in opposite directions — surfacing the tension as voice rather than as a number on a spreadsheet.

Funding choices are themselves a kind of inventory: you allocate across departments, and that allocation regulates which mandates you can form and what insight you have into how each part of the city actually works.

---

## Policy Synergies (the heart of replay)

Policies are not isolated effects. Like Balatro jokers, **their pairing and accumulation is where the game lives.**

- **Welfare without income** → you bleed more money than tax brings in
- **Housing without transit/regulation** → prices skyrocket → unrest rises
- **Housing + Health** → community clinics inside affordable housing (trust gain)
- **Housing + Police** → surveillance in low-income neighborhoods (money up, trust down, hidden Surveillance climbs)
- **Education + Community Networks + high Trust** → a *Call to Aid* network: distributed 3D printers across the city can be repurposed during the disaster into emergency medical-supply fabrication

That last one is the model for the whole endgame: **synergies are not just consequences, they are prerequisites.** You need enough Community Power to deploy the printers. You need low enough Surveillance that people trust the infrastructure. You are, across many terms, *architecting what survives.*

## Run Structure & Progression

- A **term = one run = 12 weeks** (≈36 phase-decisions).
- At the end of a term, the **board resets** — like Balatro, you replay the cycle.
- Most progress is wiped, but **rare, hard-won unlocks carry forward**: a discovered policy, a cultivated community leader, an institutional capability, a landmark. These are the things that *endure*, and they are deliberately harder and rarer to find.
- The **100-year countdown** is the meta-progression. Each term advances you toward the disaster, and the crises escalate as you go.
- Persistent unlocks change *future* runs (Balatro's logic): solving a crisis once may open a new department or policy next term — or leave a scar that makes things harder.

---

## What Carries Forward (the emotional core)

The question of **what survives a reset** is the soul of the game. Candidates:

- **Community leaders** you cultivated — they return across terms with relationships, grudges, and skills, like roguelike relics. Your coalition is different every playthrough.
- **Institutional memory** — capabilities the city "remembers" how to do.
- **Landmarks and infrastructure** — physical things that persist.

A city is not just its wealth or its policies. It is the **people who endured and the bonds that held.** That is what you carry to the disaster.

---

## Replayability Summary

Each run feels distinct because of compounding non-determinism:

1. **Which meetings surface** (rule-governed randomness)
2. **Which constituencies you choose** to spend time on (hard scarcity)
3. **Which insights you happen to surface** (per-conversation chance)
4. **Which policy synergies emerge** from your unlocked palette
5. **Which hidden meters you trip** without meaning to
6. **Which rare unlocks** you carry into the next term

You are never executing the same strategy twice — you are *responding to the city you happened to listen to.*
