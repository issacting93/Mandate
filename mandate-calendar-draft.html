1Slots on top, deal below

Committed meetings sit in the three slots up top (face-up, full detail). The dealt hand of district cards sits below — the draft reads top-down: here's what I've taken, here's what's on offer.

2Face-down ≠ blank

An unvisited card still shows borough, bloc, a feed rumor, and which lens would read it — enough to make picking it a decision, not a coinflip. The hatched back signals "you don't know who's here yet."

3The lens reads the deck

Bushwick's card is outlined and reveals a fragility hint because Safety is funded to competence. Flushing stays dim because Infrastructure isn't. This is the single best tie between the money zone and the draft — funding perception literally improves your draft information.

4Re-deal has a price

Don't like the hand? Re-deal — but it costs a slot, so a bad week is a real loss, not a free reroll. (Blue Prince's "you live with the draw.") Tunable: cost, and whether re-deal is allowed at all early-game.

What this changes in the GDD (not just the UI)

§5.4 / §4 — the deal is now a system. The week deals a hand from a weighted pool (wanted-marked districts, feed-hot districts, neglected districts drifting in). Map-labelling becomes "mark as wanted" → higher deal odds, not guaranteed placement. Needs a small DealSystem (or a mode of ScenarioSystem) with the weighting rules. Face-down legibility ties to DepartmentSystem. A card's back reveals more when its coded lens is funded to competence — so DepartmentSystem now also answers "how legible is this dealt card?", not just conversation interjections and bento tiles. One more payoff on the same spine. Open question: what fills the hand when you've visited everything? Late-game, most cards flip face-up and the draft becomes about freshness/trust decay rather than mystery — which may be the right arc, or may need re-mystifying via new fragilities (the roguelike reshuffle, §5.13).
